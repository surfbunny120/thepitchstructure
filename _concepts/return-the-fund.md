## Why It Matters

*Return the Fund* demonstrates a terminal optimization within the venture capital community that forces more dollars to fewer players and leaves less room for a wide variety of successful business outcomes. If every company must be a unicorn to be a viable investment, what does that mean for the rest of us? Is there room in our society for businesses that do not become behemoths? And if not, what are the consequences?

## Sources Consulted

**Money going to fewer players:**

- **Carta: VC Dollars Grow More Concentrated Amid Fewer Deals** — This is probably your best single source. In 2024, deals dropped 7% while cash raised jumped 18%. Fewer companies, bigger checks. Average round size up 28% in one year. And on the fund side: 9 firms raised nearly 50% of all US VC capital.

- **SaaStr: Venture Has Never Been More Concentrated — 40%+ of VC $$$ Going to Just 10 Deals** — The headline says it. $81.3B to 10 startups out of $197.2B total. Three AI companies alone got $65B — a third of all venture dollars.

- **VC Cafe: The Concentration of Venture Capital Money in 2025** — 8% of deals accounted for 75% of all VC dollars in Q4 2025.

- **SSTI: The State of US Venture Capital in Four Charts** — Good charts showing deal count cratering (69% drop from 2022 highs) while dollar volume climbs. The visual version of the argument.

**The herd mentality / same bets angle:**

- **PitchBook: Herd Mentality is Alive and Well as VCs Chase AI Craze** — Directly addresses VCs all piling into the same bets. Quotes a partner saying when something gets hot there's a "massive push to be involved."

- **IMD: Decoding the Behavioral Biases That Influence Venture Capital Funds** — Academic take on why VCs follow each other. Uses WeWork and Bird as cautionary examples of herd-driven overvaluation.

**The inequality / who benefits angle:**

- **Penn Today: 'Behind the Startup' Looks at Venture Capital and Inequality** — Sociologist Benjamin Shestakofsky's book argues the VC system is designed to funnel rewards to elites. Suggests alternative models (worker cooperatives, nonprofits). This one connects VC structure directly to inequality.

- **Harvard Law: Venture Capital Outlook for 2026 — 5 Key Trends** — From Harvard's corporate governance forum. Treats concentration as structural, not cyclical.

- **Next Big Idea Club: Why Venture Capital is Failing at Innovation** — Directly argues the VC model now harms innovation rather than fostering it.

**The "return the fund" power law specifically:**

- **The VC Factory: Understanding Power Law — Do VCs Take Enough Risks?** — Explains the "return the fund" logic: a $100M fund needs a $500M exit at 20% ownership just to break even. This forces VCs to only back potential unicorns, which means they all chase the same companies.

- **The VC Factory: The "Super Power Law" in Venture Capital** — Goes further: the power law applies not just to startups but to funds themselves. 1.1% of startups returned the value of the entire fund that invested in them.

---

*The stat I'd lead with: In 2025, 8% of venture deals got 75% of all venture dollars. Three companies got a third of everything. That's the return-the-fund logic playing out in real time — VCs aren't funding innovation broadly, they're all making the same bet on the same handful of companies, because that's the only math that works for their fund structure.*
